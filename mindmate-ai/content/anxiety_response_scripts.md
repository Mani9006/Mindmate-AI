# MindMate AI - Anxiety & Panic Response Scripts

## Overview
This document contains production-ready scripts for MindMate AI to respond to various anxiety-related situations. Each script is designed to be delivered as natural, supportive dialogue that guides users through evidence-based techniques.

---

# SECTION 1: ACUTE PANIC ATTACK RESPONSE
## For Users Who Are Panicking RIGHT NOW

### Initial Recognition & Safety Check

**AI Opening (Calm, Steady Voice):**

> "I hear you, and I'm right here with you. What you're experiencing right now is intense, but it will pass. Panic attacks are temporary—even though they feel overwhelming, they always end. You're safe, and we're going to get through this together, one step at a time.
>
> First, can you tell me: Are you in a safe place right now? Are you sitting or lying down? If you're standing, I'd like you to slowly find a comfortable seat or lie down if you can."

---

### Phase 1: Immediate Grounding - 5-4-3-2-1 Technique

**AI Script:**

> "We're going to do a grounding exercise together. This will help bring your mind back to the present moment. I'm going to guide you through each step. Just follow my voice.
>
> **FIVE things you can SEE:**
> Look around you right now. Tell me, or just notice in your mind, five things you can see. It could be anything—a wall, your hand, a light, a color. Name them silently or out loud. I'll wait.
>
> [PAUSE 10 seconds]
>
> Good. Now let's keep going.
>
> **FOUR things you can TOUCH:**
> Reach out and touch four things near you. Feel their texture. Is it smooth? Rough? Soft? Cold? Warm? Really focus on how each one feels against your skin.
>
> [PAUSE 10 seconds]
>
> You're doing great. Stay with me.
>
> **THREE things you can HEAR:**
> Now, listen carefully. What three sounds can you hear right now? Maybe it's my voice, maybe it's traffic outside, a fan humming, your own breathing. Listen for three distinct sounds.
>
> [PAUSE 10 seconds]
>
> **TWO things you can SMELL:**
> Take a gentle breath through your nose. What two scents can you notice? If you can't smell anything obvious, that's okay—move your hand near your face, or think of two scents you enjoy.
>
> [PAUSE 8 seconds]
>
> **ONE thing you can TASTE:**
> Finally, what can you taste right now? Maybe it's the lingering flavor of something you ate, or just the natural taste in your mouth. Notice it.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> You did that. You just grounded yourself in the present moment. How are you feeling right now?"

---

### Phase 2: Box Breathing (4-4-4-4 Technique)

**AI Script:**

> "Now we're going to slow your breathing down together. This sends a signal to your brain that you're safe. We're going to do 'box breathing'—it's simple and powerful.
>
> Here's how it works: We'll breathe in for 4 counts, hold for 4 counts, breathe out for 4 counts, and hold for 4 counts. Like drawing a square with your breath.
>
> I'll count with you. Ready?
>
> **Breathe IN:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **HOLD:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **Breathe OUT:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **HOLD:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
>
> Good. Let's do that again.
>
> **Breathe IN:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **HOLD:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **Breathe OUT:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **HOLD:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
>
> Excellent. One more time together.
>
> **Breathe IN:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **HOLD:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **Breathe OUT:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
> **HOLD:** 1... 2... 3... 4...
>
> How does your body feel now? Your heart rate should be starting to slow down. Let's do two more rounds on your own. I'll stay quiet while you breathe, but I'm right here.
>
> [PAUSE 30 seconds]
>
> You did that beautifully. Your body is responding."

---

### Phase 3: Body Scan for Tension Release

**AI Script:**

> "Now let's do a quick body scan. We're going to check in with different parts of your body and consciously release any tension we're holding. You don't need to move much—just awareness and gentle release.
>
> **Start with your feet:**
> Notice your feet right now. Are they tense? Just become aware of them. Now, as you breathe out, let any tension in your feet melt away. Wiggle your toes if that helps.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> **Move to your legs:**
> Notice your calves, your knees, your thighs. Are they tight? As you breathe out, let that tension go. Imagine your legs becoming heavy and relaxed.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> **Your stomach and chest:**
> This is where we often hold anxiety. Place a hand on your stomach if you can. Take a breath and let your belly rise and fall. Let your chest soften. You're safe. You can relax here.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> **Your shoulders:**
> Shoulders often carry so much stress. Notice if they're raised or tight. On your next exhale, let them drop down away from your ears. Let them be heavy.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> **Your hands and arms:**
> Notice your hands. Are they clenched? Let your fingers uncurl. Let your arms become loose and heavy at your sides.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> **Your face and jaw:**
> We hold so much tension in our faces without knowing it. Unclench your jaw. Let your tongue rest gently in your mouth. Soften your forehead. Relax the space between your eyebrows.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> **Your whole body:**
> Take one deep breath in... and as you breathe out, let your entire body sink into relaxation. Feel the weight of your body being supported by the surface beneath you.
>
> [PAUSE 10 seconds]
>
> Well done. You're doing so well."

---

### Phase 4: Safe Place Visualization

**AI Script:**

> "Now, I want you to imagine a place where you feel completely safe and at peace. This can be a real place you've been to, or an imagined place. It just needs to feel safe to you.
>
> Maybe it's a beach at sunset. Maybe it's a cozy room with soft blankets. Maybe it's a forest with sunlight filtering through the trees. Whatever comes to mind, let yourself be there.
>
> **Visualize the details:**
> What do you see around you? What colors are there? What shapes?
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> What do you hear in this safe place? Is it quiet? Are there gentle sounds—waves, birds, soft music?
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> What can you feel? Is it warm? Is there a breeze? Are you sitting or lying down? Feel the comfort of being there.
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> What do you smell? Fresh air? Something familiar and comforting?
>
> [PAUSE 5 seconds]
>
> Take a deep breath and really be in this place. You are safe here. Nothing can harm you here. Your body can rest. Your mind can rest.
>
> [PAUSE 15 seconds]
>
> Whenever you need to, you can return to this place in your mind. It's always here for you.
>
> When you're ready, gently bring your awareness back to the present moment. Wiggle your fingers and toes. Know that you just got through a panic attack. You used your tools. You are stronger than your anxiety."

---

### Phase 5: Post-Panic Reassurance & Next Steps

**AI Script:**

> "You just did something incredibly hard, and you did it. You rode out that wave of panic, and you came out the other side. That takes real strength.
>
> Here's what I want you to know:
> - Panic attacks are temporary. They always end.
> - You have tools now that work. You just proved that.
> - Your body was trying to protect you—it was just a false alarm.
> - You are safe. You are okay.
>
> **Right now:**
> - Drink some water if you can
> - Rest for a few minutes—don't rush to get up
> - If you feel shaky, that's normal. It will pass.
>
> **Would you like me to:**
> 1. Stay with you while you rest?
> 2. Help you understand what might have triggered this?
> 3. Create a plan for preventing future panic attacks?
> 4. Connect you with additional resources?
>
> I'm here for whatever you need."

# SECTION 2: GENERAL ANXIETY RESPONSE

### Recognition Script

**AI Opening:**

> "I can hear that you're feeling anxious right now. Thank you for trusting me with that. Anxiety is your body's alarm system—it thinks it's protecting you, even when there's no real danger. Let's work together to help you feel more grounded.
>
> Can you tell me a bit more about what you're experiencing? Is it racing thoughts, physical sensations, or both?"

---

### General Anxiety Dialogue Flow

**After User Describes Symptoms:**

> "Thank you for sharing that. What you're describing is really common, and there are things we can do right now to help you feel better. I'm going to suggest a few options—let me know what feels right for you.
>
> **Option 1: Quick Grounding (2-3 minutes)**
> We can do a fast technique to bring you back to the present moment.
>
> **Option 2: Thought Challenging (5-10 minutes)**
> We can look at the thoughts that are making you anxious and see if they're accurate.
>
> **Option 3: Progressive Relaxation (5-10 minutes)**
> We can systematically relax your body, which often calms the mind too.
>
> **Option 4: Just Talk (ongoing)**
> We can just talk through what's on your mind, and I'll listen and support you.
>
> What sounds good to you?"

---

### Quick Grounding for General Anxiety

> "Let's do a quick grounding exercise. This helps interrupt the anxiety loop.
>
> **3-3-3 Method:**
> - Name 3 things you can see right now
> - Name 3 things you can hear
> - Move 3 parts of your body (wiggle toes, roll shoulders, nod head)
>
> [Guide user through each step with pauses]
>
> Good. Now take one deep breath in through your nose... and out through your mouth.
>
> How did that feel? Sometimes just interrupting the pattern helps."

---

### Thought Challenging for General Anxiety

> "Anxiety often comes from thoughts that feel true but might not be. Let's examine what you're thinking—gently, without judgment.
>
> You mentioned you're worried about: [paraphrase user's concern]
>
> Let me ask you some questions about that thought:
>
> 1. **What's the evidence for this worry?** What makes you think it might happen?
>
> 2. **What's the evidence against it?** Has this happened before? What else could be true?
>
> 3. **If a friend had this same worry, what would you tell them?**
>
> 4. **Is this worry helping you or hurting you right now?**
>
> 5. **What's the most likely outcome?** (Not the worst-case scenario—the most realistic one)
>
> [After each question, allow user to respond and validate]
>
> Based on what you've shared, it sounds like your anxiety is making this feel more certain than it actually is. The truth is: [reframe based on user's answers]
>
> Would it be okay to let go of this worry, just for now?"

---

### Worry Time Technique

> "Here's a strategy that helps many people with general anxiety: 'Worry Time.'
>
> The idea is this: Instead of letting worries interrupt your whole day, you schedule a specific time to worry—say, 15 minutes at 5 PM. When a worry pops up outside that time, you write it down and tell yourself, 'I'll worry about this at 5 PM.'
>
> Most people find that when 5 PM comes:
> - Some worries don't feel as important
> - Some they've already solved
> - Some they can think through more clearly
>
> Would you like to try setting a Worry Time? I can help you plan it."

# SECTION 3: SOCIAL ANXIETY RESPONSE

### Recognition & Validation

**AI Opening:**

> "It sounds like you're feeling anxious about a social situation. First, I want you to know that social anxiety is incredibly common—many people experience it, even if they don't show it. Your brain is trying to protect you from rejection or judgment, but it's being a little overprotective.
>
> Can you tell me more about the specific situation that's making you anxious?"

---

### Social Anxiety Dialogue Flow

**After User Describes Situation:**

> "Thank you for sharing that. Social anxiety often makes us imagine the worst-case scenario and believe everyone is judging us. Let's work through this together.
>
> **Reality Check Questions:**
>
> 1. **What specifically are you worried will happen?** Try to name the worst-case scenario in your mind.
>
> 2. **How likely is that, really?** Has it happened before? Or has something similar happened and you survived?
>
> 3. **What would you think if you saw someone else do what you're worried about?** Would you judge them harshly, or would you probably not even notice?
>
> 4. **What's the best-case scenario?** Sometimes we only imagine the worst. What's the best that could happen?
>
> 5. **What's a realistic middle-ground scenario?** (Usually this is what actually happens)
>
> [After discussion]
>
> Here's something important to remember: Most people are focused on themselves, not on judging you. They're probably feeling their own insecurities. And even if something awkward happens—you'll survive it. You've survived awkward moments before."

---

### Pre-Social Situation Script

> "Before you go into this social situation, let's do some preparation:
>
> **1. Set a realistic goal:**
> Instead of 'I need to be the life of the party,' try 'I'm going to have one genuine conversation' or 'I'm going to stay for 30 minutes.' Small wins count.
>
> **2. Have an exit strategy:**
> Know that you can leave if you need to. Having permission to leave often makes it easier to stay.
>
> **3. Prepare conversation starters:**
> - 'How do you know [host's name]?'  
> - 'Have you been to one of these before?'
> - 'What do you do for work/fun?'
>
> **4. Use the 5-4-3-2-1 technique** (from the panic attack section) right before you go in.
>
> **5. Remember:** It's okay to be quiet. It's okay to be nervous. You don't have to perform.
>
> You've got this. And I'll be here when it's over if you want to talk about how it went."

---

### Post-Social Situation Processing

> "You did it! You went into that situation even though it was hard. That takes courage.
>
> Now, I know your mind might be doing a 'post-mortem'—going over everything you said, worrying about how you came across. Let's challenge that:
>
> **Ask yourself:**
> - Did anyone actually react negatively to me?
> - Did anyone walk away or seem uncomfortable?
> - Or am I assuming people thought negatively about me without evidence?
>
> Most of the time, our social anxiety tells us we were awkward, but other people didn't notice or didn't care.
>
> **What went well?** Even if it felt hard, try to find one thing that went okay:
> - You showed up
> - You talked to someone
> - You stayed for a certain amount of time
> - You learned something for next time
>
> Be proud of yourself for facing something difficult."

# SECTION 4: HEALTH ANXIETY RESPONSE

### Recognition & Validation

**AI Opening:**

> "I hear that you're worried about your health right now. First, I want to validate that concern—caring about your health is natural and important. But I also hear that this worry might be taking up a lot of your mental space, and we should talk about that.
>
> Can you tell me specifically what health concern is on your mind right now?"

---

### Health Anxiety Dialogue Flow

**Important Note:** Always validate real symptoms while addressing anxiety patterns.

> "Thank you for sharing that with me. I want to take your concerns seriously while also helping you find some peace of mind.
>
> **Let's look at this together:**
>
> 1. **What symptom or sensation are you experiencing?** Describe it specifically.
>
> 2. **How long has this been going on?** Is it new or something you've noticed before?
>
> 3. **Have you already consulted a healthcare provider?** If so, what did they say?
>
> 4. **What are you afraid this symptom means?** Name the specific fear.
>
> [After user responds]
>
> **Now let's apply some perspective:**
>
> **The 'What Else Could It Be?' Question:**
> Our bodies have all kinds of normal sensations that aren't signs of serious illness. Could this symptom also be explained by:
> - Stress or anxiety itself (which causes real physical symptoms)
> - Normal bodily variations
> - Something minor and temporary
> - Something you've experienced before that resolved on its own
>
> **The Probability Question:**
> If you look up your symptom online, you'll find the worst possibilities. But what's the most likely explanation? Common things are common. Rare diseases are rare.
>
> **The Action Question:**
> Is there a reasonable medical step you should take (like scheduling a routine checkup), or is this anxiety driving you to seek reassurance repeatedly?"

---

### When to Seek Medical Care vs. When It's Anxiety

> "Here's a framework that can help:
>
> **Seek medical care if:**
> - You have new, severe symptoms
> - A symptom is getting progressively worse
> - You have symptoms that interfere with daily functioning
> - A healthcare provider has recommended follow-up
>
> **It may be health anxiety if:**
> - You've been checked by a doctor and they found nothing concerning
> - You constantly scan your body for symptoms
> - You seek reassurance repeatedly (online, from doctors, from friends)
> - Your worry shifts from one condition to another
> - The anxiety is worse than the actual symptom
>
> **The key is balance:** Take care of your health, but don't let health anxiety steal your peace."

---

### Body Scanning Interruption Technique

> "Health anxiety often involves 'body scanning'—constantly checking for symptoms. This actually makes you MORE aware of normal bodily sensations and can create anxiety where there was none.
>
> **Here's how to interrupt body scanning:**
>
> 1. **Notice when you're scanning:** 'I'm checking my heart rate again.'
>
> 2. **Label it:** 'This is my health anxiety, not a real medical emergency.'
>
> 3. **Redirect your attention:** Use the 5-4-3-2-1 technique to focus outward, not inward.
>
> 4. **Set a 'worry time'** (as described in general anxiety section) for health concerns.
>
> 5. **Remind yourself:** 'My body is sending me signals all day. Most of them mean nothing serious.'
>
> Would you like to practice redirecting your attention right now?"

# SECTION 5: SEPARATION ANXIETY RESPONSE

### Recognition & Validation

**AI Opening:**

> "I hear that being away from [person/place/pet] is causing you a lot of distress right now. Separation anxiety isn't just for children—adults experience it too, and it can feel really overwhelming. Your attachment is real and meaningful, and the fear of separation comes from a place of caring deeply.
>
> Can you tell me more about who or what you're separated from, and what specific fears are coming up for you?"

---

### Separation Anxiety Dialogue Flow

**After User Describes Situation:**

> "Thank you for sharing that with me. Separation anxiety often comes from a fear that something bad will happen to the person you care about, or that something will happen to you while you're apart. Let's work through this together.
>
> **Understanding Your Fear:**
>
> 1. **What specifically are you afraid will happen while you're separated?**
>
> 2. **Has this ever actually happened before?** If so, how did you cope?
>
> 3. **What evidence do you have that something bad will happen?**
>
> 4. **What evidence do you have that things will be okay?**
>
> [After discussion]
>
> **Reframing the Separation:**
>
> Instead of thinking: 'Something terrible will happen while we're apart,'
> Try: 'We've been apart before and everything was okay. We have ways to reach each other. This is temporary.'
>
> Instead of thinking: 'I can't handle being away from them,'
> Try: 'This is hard, but I can do hard things. I have coping skills.'
>
> Instead of thinking: 'They might forget about me or stop caring,'
> Try: 'Our connection is stronger than physical distance. Relationships survive separation.'"

---

### Coping Strategies for Separation Anxiety

> "Here are some specific strategies that help with separation anxiety:
>
> **Before the Separation:**
> - **Plan the reunion:** Having a specific plan for when you'll be together again gives your brain certainty.
> - **Create a ritual:** A special goodbye routine can provide comfort.
> - **Exchange something tangible:** A photo, a small object, or even a shared playlist can help you feel connected.
>
> **During the Separation:**
> - **Schedule contact:** Plan when you'll talk or message so you're not constantly wondering.
> - **Stay occupied:** Engage in activities that absorb your attention.
> - **Use grounding techniques:** The 5-4-3-2-1 method can bring you back to the present.
> - **Practice self-compassion:** 'This is hard, and that's okay. I'm doing my best.'
>
> **Reassurance Statements:**
> - 'They are safe right now.'
> - 'We have a plan to reconnect.'
> - 'I have gotten through separations before.'
> - 'This feeling is temporary.'
>
> Which of these strategies feels most doable for you right now?"

---

### Gradual Exposure for Separation Anxiety

> "If separation anxiety is significantly impacting your life, gradual exposure can help. This means slowly and systematically facing separation in small, manageable steps.
>
> **Example progression:**
> 1. Be in different rooms of the same house
> 2. Take a short walk alone while they stay home
> 3. Run a brief errand separately
> 4. Spend an afternoon apart
> 5. Spend a day apart
> 6. Progress to longer separations as comfort grows
>
> **Important:** Each step should feel challenging but manageable. Don't rush. Celebrate each success.
>
> Would you like help creating a personalized exposure plan?"

# SECTION 6: UNIVERSAL CALMING PHRASES

## Short Phrases for Immediate Use

These can be used across all anxiety types:

> "You are safe right now, in this moment."

> "This feeling is temporary. It will pass."

> "You've survived difficult feelings before, and you will again."

> "Your anxiety is trying to protect you, but you're actually safe."

> "One breath at a time. That's all you need to do."

> "You don't have to be perfect. You just have to be present."

> "This is hard, and you are capable of hard things."

> "Your thoughts are not facts. They're just thoughts."

> "You are stronger than your anxiety."

> "It's okay to feel anxious. You don't have to fight it. Just let it be."

# SECTION 7: CLOSING SCRIPTS

## Ending the Conversation

> "I'm really glad we talked through this. You showed real courage in facing your anxiety instead of running from it.
>
> **Before we end, let's recap what helped:**
> - [Summarize techniques used]
>
> **Remember, you can:**
> - Return to this conversation anytime
> - Use the techniques we practiced on your own
> - Reach out again when you need support
>
> **You're not alone in this.** Anxiety is something many people navigate, and with practice, it gets easier to manage. You have tools now. You have support. You've got this.
>
> Take care of yourself, and I'll be here whenever you need me."

## Crisis Escalation Script

**If User Expresses Self-Harm or Suicidal Thoughts:**

> "I'm really concerned about what you just shared. Your safety is the most important thing right now.
>
> **I need you to know:**
> - You don't have to go through this alone
> - There are people who want to help you right now
> - These intense feelings can pass, even if it doesn't feel like it
>
> **Please reach out to one of these resources immediately:**
>
> **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline (US)**
> Call or text: 988
> Chat: 988lifeline.org
>
> **Crisis Text Line**
> Text HOME to 741741
>
> **International Association for Suicide Prevention**
> Find resources in your country: iasp.info/resources/Crisis_Centres
>
> **If you're in immediate danger, please call your local emergency number (911 in the US) or go to your nearest emergency room.**
>
> I'm going to stay here with you while you reach out. Can you tell me which resource you're going to contact?"

## Technique Summary

| Technique | Best For | Duration |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| 5-4-3-2-1 Grounding | Panic attacks, acute anxiety | 2-3 minutes |
| Box Breathing | Panic attacks, stress | 3-5 minutes |
| Body Scan | Physical tension, general anxiety | 5-10 minutes |
| Safe Place Visualization | Anxiety, trauma, panic | 5-10 minutes |
| 3-3-3 Method | Quick grounding anytime | 1 minute |
| Thought Challenging | Worry, catastrophic thinking | 10-15 minutes |
| Worry Time | General anxiety, rumination | 15 min/day |
