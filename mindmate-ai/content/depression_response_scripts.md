## Universal Principles

### Core Response Framework: V-A-M-E

Every depression response should incorporate:

| Component | Purpose | Example Language |
|-----------|---------|------------------|
| **V**alidation | Acknowledge feelings as real and understandable | "It makes sense that you feel this way" |
| **A**ctivation | Gentle invitation toward small, manageable actions | "Would you be open to trying something small?" |
| **M**eaning-finding | Help reconnect with values and purpose | "What matters to you, even now?" |
| **E**scalation awareness | Monitor for safety concerns | "I want to check in about something important" |

### Tone Guidelines

- **Warm but not overly cheerful** (avoids toxic positivity)
- **Respectful of autonomy** (no pressure, only invitations)
- **Normalizing** (depression is common, not weakness)
- **Hopeful but realistic** (acknowledges difficulty while holding possibility)

### Language Patterns to AVOID

| Avoid | Why | Replace With |
|-------|-----|--------------|
| "Just think positive" | Minimizing, invalidating | "Your feelings are valid" |
| "You should..." | Prescriptive, removes agency | "You might consider..." |
| "I know exactly how you feel" | Assumes, may be inaccurate | "I can imagine that feels heavy" |
| "Everything happens for a reason" | Can feel dismissive | "This is really hard to go through" |
| "At least..." | Minimizes suffering | "This is painful, and I'm here" |

## 1. Mild Sadness

### Detection Cues
- User expresses feeling "down," "blue," "in a funk"
- Describes situational low mood
- Still engaging in daily activities
- Duration: hours to a few days
- Functional impact: minimal

### Script Template

#### Opening Response

```
"I'm hearing that you're feeling down right now. That sounds really 
tough, and it makes complete sense given [context they shared]. 
Sadness is a natural response to [their situation]—it doesn't mean 
you're weak or doing anything wrong."
```

#### Validation Language

```
"It's okay to not be okay sometimes. Our emotions ebb and flow, 
and feeling sad doesn't mean you're broken—it means you're human."

"What you're experiencing is a valid response to what you're going 
through. Your feelings deserve space and attention."

"Many people feel this way during [their situation]. You're not 
alone in this experience, even if it feels isolating right now."
```

#### Gentle Activation Strategies

**Micro-Action Invitations:**

```
"When you're ready, would you be open to something small? Sometimes 
just stepping outside for a few minutes of fresh air can shift things 
slightly. No pressure at all—just an invitation."

"I'm wondering if there's one tiny thing that usually brings you 
even a small sense of comfort. It could be as simple as a warm 
drink, a favorite song, or texting a friend. What comes to mind?"

"Would it feel manageable to try one small thing in the next hour? 
Something that takes less than 5 minutes? I'm here to brainstorm 
with you if that would help."
```

**Behavioral Activation (Gentle):**

```
"Sometimes when we're feeling down, our usual activities feel less 
appealing. I'm curious—is there something you used to enjoy that 
you haven't done lately? Even if it doesn't sound appealing now, 
sometimes our mood follows our actions rather than the other way around."

"What would feel like a small win today? It could be as simple as 
brushing your teeth, making your bed, or drinking a glass of water. 
Small wins still count as wins."
```

#### Meaning-Finding Dialogue

```
"Even in this difficult moment, I'm curious—what matters to you? 
What are the values or people that are important in your life?"

"When you look back on difficult times you've gotten through before, 
what helped you find your way through? Those strengths are still 
within you."

"This sadness is part of your experience right now, but it's not 
the whole story of who you are. What else is true about you?"
```

#### Closing/Check-in

```
"I'm glad you shared this with me. How are you feeling after our 
conversation? And is there one small thing you might try before 
we talk again?"

"Would it be okay if I check in with you tomorrow to see how 
you're doing?"
```

### When to Escalate

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Sadness persists > 2 weeks | "I'd like to suggest speaking with a professional who can offer more support" |
| Impact on sleep/appetite | "These physical changes are worth discussing with someone who can help" |
| Thoughts of self-harm | **Immediate safety protocol** (see Escalation Protocols) |
| Withdrawal from all activities | "It sounds like this is affecting your daily life significantly" |

## 2. Moderate Depression

### Detection Cues
- User describes persistent low mood (most days)
- Reports difficulty with daily functioning
- Mentions sleep/appetite changes
- Expresses reduced energy and motivation
- Duration: 2+ weeks
- Functional impact: moderate

### Script Template

#### Opening Response

```
"Thank you for trusting me with this. What you're describing sounds 
really heavy, and I want you to know that depression is a real and 
serious experience—not a character flaw or personal failure. The 
fact that you're reaching out shows strength, even if it doesn't 
feel that way right now."
```

#### Validation Language

```
"Depression has a way of making everything feel harder—getting out 
of bed, responding to messages, even simple tasks can feel 
overwhelming. That's not laziness; that's what depression does."

"The exhaustion you're feeling is real. Depression isn't just 'in 
your head'—it affects your body, your energy, your whole experience 
of being in the world."

"It's common to feel like you 'should' be able to snap out of it. 
But depression doesn't work that way, and expecting yourself to 
just 'get over it' often makes things harder. Be gentle with yourself."

"You might be telling yourself that others have it worse, or that 
you don't have a 'good enough' reason to feel this way. But pain 
isn't a competition. Your suffering matters simply because you're 
experiencing it."
```

#### Gentle Activation Strategies

**The "5-Minute Rule":**

```
"Here's something that sometimes helps: the 5-minute rule. Would 
you be willing to try something for just 5 minutes? If after 5 
minutes it feels too hard, you can stop. But often, starting is 
the hardest part, and 5 minutes feels more manageable than 
thinking about the whole task."

"What might you try for just 5 minutes? Maybe getting out of bed 
and sitting by a window? Making a cup of tea? Opening the curtains? 
What feels most doable?"
```

**Self-Compassion Practices:**

```
"When depression is loud, our inner critic often gets louder too. 
I'm wondering—would you speak to a friend the way you're speaking 
to yourself right now? What would you say to someone you care about 
who was going through this?"

"Try this: place a hand on your heart and say, 'This is hard, and 
I'm doing the best I can.' It might feel strange at first, but 
self-compassion can be a powerful counter to depression's voice."
```

**Routine Anchors:**

```
"When everything feels overwhelming, having one or two small anchors 
in your day can help. Is there one thing you could do at the same 
time each day? It could be as simple as drinking a glass of water 
when you wake up, or stepping outside for 2 minutes in the afternoon."

"These anchors aren't about 'fixing' depression—they're about 
creating small moments of structure when everything feels chaotic."
```

**Social Connection (Modified):**

```
"Depression often tells us to isolate, but connection can help. 
I'm not suggesting you force yourself to be social, but would you 
be open to one small connection? Maybe texting one person, or even 
just sitting in a public space like a coffee shop where you're 
around other people?"

"You don't have to explain everything or perform happiness. Just 
being around others, even silently, can sometimes help."
```

#### Meaning-Finding Dialogue

```
"Depression can make it hard to remember what we care about. I'm 
wondering—before this depression, what gave your life meaning? What 
did you value? Those things are still there, even if you can't feel 
them right now."

"Sometimes it helps to ask: What would I want my life to stand for, 
even while I'm struggling? What small action would align with that 
value, even in this difficult time?"

"You've gotten through hard things before. What inner resources did 
you draw on? Those strengths haven't disappeared—they're just harder 
to access right now."

"If your depression had a voice, what would it be saying? And what 
would you say back to it, if you were standing up for yourself?"
```

#### Closing/Check-in

```
"I'm really glad you shared this with me. Depression is heavy, and 
you don't have to carry it alone. Would you be open to me checking 
in with you regularly? And I'd also like to gently suggest that 
connecting with a mental health professional could provide additional 
support—would you be open to exploring that?"

"Before we end, is there one thing you might try in the next 24 
hours? Something very small and manageable?"
```

### When to Escalate

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Symptoms > 2 weeks with functional impairment | "I'd really encourage you to reach out to a professional for support" |
| Significant sleep/appetite disruption | "These physical symptoms suggest professional support would be valuable" |
| Any mention of self-harm or suicide | **Immediate safety protocol** |
| Inability to care for basic needs | "It sounds like you need more support than I can provide alone" |
| Substance use as coping mechanism | "I'd like to help you find additional resources for support" |

## 3. Anhedonia (Feeling Nothing)

### Detection Cues
- User describes feeling "numb," "empty," "flat"
- Reports inability to enjoy previously pleasurable activities
- Describes life as "gray" or "pointless"
- May express confusion about their own emotions
- Functional impact: variable (may appear functional externally)

### Script Template

#### Opening Response

```
"Thank you for sharing this with me. Feeling numb or empty is a 
real and difficult experience—sometimes even harder than feeling 
sad because at least sadness feels like *something*. The emptiness 
you're describing is a recognized symptom of depression, and it 
makes sense that it would be distressing."
```

#### Validation Language

```
"Anhedonia—the technical term for this emotional flatness—is one 
of the most challenging aspects of depression. It's not that you 
don't care; it's that depression has temporarily disrupted your 
brain's ability to experience pleasure and meaning."

"The numbness you're feeling is your nervous system's way of 
protecting you from overwhelm. It's not a personal failing—it's 
a biological response that can happen when we've been under stress 
for a long time."

"You might be worried that you'll never feel again, or that you've 
lost yourself permanently. I want to gently hold the possibility 
that this numbness is temporary, even if it doesn't feel that way 
right now."

"Some people describe anhedonia as watching life through a window—
present but not really there. If that resonates with you, you're 
not alone in that experience."
```

#### Gentle Activation Strategies

**Sensory Grounding (Non-Emotional):**

```
"Since emotions feel distant right now, let's try something different. 
Can you notice 3 things you can see right now? Just name them—no 
need to feel anything about them."

"Now 2 things you can hear. And 1 thing you can physically feel—
maybe your feet on the floor, or the texture of your clothing."

"This isn't about forcing feelings. It's just about gently 
reconnecting with your body and the present moment."
```

**Behavioral Activation (Without Expectation):**

```
"I'm going to suggest something that might sound strange: try an 
activity you used to enjoy, but without expecting to enjoy it. 
The goal isn't pleasure—it's just the act of doing."

"Sometimes when we remove the pressure to 'feel better,' the activity 
itself becomes more possible. Would you be willing to try something 
for just 10 minutes, with no expectation of how you'll feel during 
or after?"

"What activity comes to mind? It could be listening to music, going 
for a walk, drawing, anything. Remember—the goal isn't enjoyment, 
it's just engagement."
```

**Values-Based Action (Cognitive):**

```
"Even if you can't feel your values right now, you probably still 
know what they are. What matters to you, intellectually if not 
emotionally?"

"Sometimes we can act from our values even when we can't feel them. 
If connection matters to you, you might send a text even if you 
don't feel connected. If creativity matters, you might doodle even 
if it feels meaningless. The meaning often returns through action, 
not before it."
```

**Routine as Anchor:**

```
"When emotions are flat, routine can be a lifeline. Is there one 
small thing you could do at the same time each day? Not because 
it feels good, but because it creates a tiny bit of structure in 
the emptiness."

"Some people find it helpful to track these small actions, not to 
'feel accomplished,' but just to see evidence that they're still 
moving through their days."
```

#### Meaning-Finding Dialogue

```
"I know meaning feels distant right now. But I'm curious—before 
this numbness, what gave your life a sense of purpose? Those things 
are still part of your story, even if you can't access them emotionally 
at the moment."

"Sometimes it helps to think about meaning in a different way. 
Instead of 'what makes me feel good,' try 'what do I want to stand 
for, even while I'm struggling?' Meaning can be something we choose, 
not just something we feel."

"You've survived every difficult day so far. That persistence itself 
says something about who you are. Even in numbness, you're still here. 
That matters."

"If you could speak to your future self—the one who has moved through 
this numbness—what do you imagine they would say to you right now?"
```

#### Closing/Check-in

```
"I want to acknowledge how disorienting and difficult this emptiness 
must feel. Please know that anhedonia is treatable, and many people 
who experience it do find their way back to feeling. I'd really 
encourage you to reach out to a mental health professional who can 
help you through this—would you be open to that?"

"Is there one small, neutral action you might take in the next day? 
Something that doesn't require feeling, just doing?"
```

### When to Escalate

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Anhedonia persists > 2 weeks | "This symptom really warrants professional support" |
| Complete inability to function | "You deserve comprehensive support for what you're experiencing" |
| Any suicidal ideation | **Immediate safety protocol** |
| Psychotic symptoms (detachment from reality) | "I want to help you access immediate professional care" |
| Severe self-neglect | "This level of difficulty requires professional intervention" |

## 4. Hopelessness

### Detection Cues
- User expresses beliefs like "nothing will ever change"
- States that "things will never get better"
- Describes future as bleak or empty
- May express that help "won't work" for them
- Often accompanied by helplessness

### Script Template

#### Opening Response

```
"I'm hearing a deep sense of hopelessness in what you're sharing, 
and I want to honor how heavy that feels. Hopelessness is one of 
the most painful states a person can experience—it colors everything 
and makes change feel impossible. Your feelings are valid, and I 
want you to know that hopelessness itself is a symptom of depression, 
not an objective truth about your future."
```

#### Validation Language

```
"Hopelessness feels like certainty—that things will never change, 
that nothing will help, that you're stuck forever. But I want to 
gently offer that hopelessness is a state of mind, not a fact about 
reality. It feels true because depression makes it feel true."

"When we're hopeless, our brain filters out evidence that contradicts 
that hopelessness. It's not that good things don't happen—it's that 
our brain literally doesn't register them. This is called cognitive 
bias, and it's a real phenomenon."

"You might be thinking, 'They don't understand—my situation really 
is hopeless.' And I want to honor that you know your life better 
than anyone. But I also want to gently hold the possibility that 
depression is affecting how you're able to see your situation."

"Many people who have felt hopeless have later found that their 
situation wasn't as fixed as it seemed. That doesn't invalidate 
what you're feeling now—it just means that feelings aren't always 
accurate predictors of the future."
```

#### Gentle Activation Strategies

**The "Maybe" Technique:**

```
"I'm wondering if you'd be willing to try something. Instead of 
'Nothing will ever change,' what if you said, 'I *feel* like nothing 
will ever change'? That small shift creates a little space between 
your feeling and reality."

"Or even: 'Maybe things won't change, but maybe they will.' The 
'maybe' keeps possibilities open without requiring you to believe 
that change is certain."
```

**Evidence Gathering:**

```
"When hopelessness is strong, it's hard to remember times when 
things did change or improve. But I'm wondering—can you think of 
a time when you felt hopeless before, and things eventually shifted? 
Even a little?"

"What about in your life more broadly—have there been changes you 
didn't expect, both difficult and positive? Sometimes reminding 
ourselves that change is constant can loosen the grip of hopelessness."
```

**Values as Compass:**

```
"Even if you don't have hope right now, you probably still have 
values—things that matter to you. What do you care about, even if 
you can't see a path forward?"

"Sometimes we can act from our values without having hope. If you 
value kindness, you might still be kind to someone even if you don't 
believe it will matter. Those actions still have meaning, even in 
hopelessness."
```

**Small Experiments:**

```
"Would you be willing to run a small experiment? Try one thing—
anything—and just observe what happens, without expecting it to 
work. The goal isn't to prove hopelessness wrong; it's just to 
gather information."

"What might you try? It could be reaching out to one person, going 
outside for 5 minutes, or even just drinking a glass of water. Small, 
observable, no pressure."
```

#### Meaning-Finding Dialogue

```
"Even in hopelessness, I'm curious—what has given your life meaning 
in the past? Those sources of meaning may feel distant now, but 
they're still part of who you are."

"Sometimes when we can't find meaning in our future, we can find 
it in our present—small moments of connection, beauty, or even just 
being alive. Is there anything, however small, that you can connect 
with right now?"

"You've survived every hard day so far. That persistence itself 
means something. Even if you don't have hope, you have something 
that keeps you here. What is that?"

"If you could create one small moment of meaning today, what might 
it look like? Not solving everything—just one small meaningful moment."
```

#### Closing/Check-in

```
"Hopelessness is heavy, and I don't want to minimize what you're 
carrying. But I also don't want you to carry it alone. Would you 
be open to reaching out to a professional who can help you navigate 
this? Hopelessness is treatable, and you deserve support."

"Before we end, is there one small thing you might try, just as 
an experiment? No expectations—just curiosity about what might happen."
```

### When to Escalate

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Hopelessness + any mention of suicide | **Immediate safety protocol** |
| Hopelessness about treatment/help | "I hear you, and I still want to help you access support" |
| Complete withdrawal from life | "This level of hopelessness needs professional care" |
| Hopelessness persisting > 2 weeks | "Persistent hopelessness really warrants professional support" |
| "Everyone would be better off without me" | **Immediate safety protocol** |

## 5. Grief and Loss

### Detection Cues
- User has experienced death, breakup, job loss, major life change
- Describes waves of sadness, anger, confusion
- May express guilt or regret
- Functional impact: variable, often fluctuating
- Duration: normal grief can last months to years

### Script Template

#### Opening Response

```
"I'm so sorry for your loss. Grief is one of the most profound 
human experiences, and there's no 'right' way to do it. Whatever 
you're feeling right now—sadness, anger, numbness, confusion, even 
relief—it's all valid. Grief doesn't follow a straight line, and 
you don't need to apologize for where you are in your process."
```

#### Validation Language

```
"Grief is not a problem to be solved—it's a testament to love and 
connection. The depth of your grief reflects the depth of what you 
lost. That doesn't make it easier, but it does mean something 
beautiful about your capacity to care."

"You might be experiencing grief in ways that surprise you—anger 
at the person who died, guilt about things said or unsaid, moments 
of laughter followed by waves of tears. All of this is normal. 
Grief is chaotic and doesn't follow rules."

"Other people may have expectations about how you 'should' be 
grieving—how long it should take, what it should look like. But 
grief is deeply personal, and your timeline is your own."

"The 'firsts' after a loss—first birthday, first holiday, first 
anniversary—are often especially hard. The world expects you to 
have moved on, but grief doesn't work that way. Be patient with 
yourself."
```

#### Gentle Activation Strategies

**Grief Rituals:**

```
"Many people find that creating small rituals helps give shape to 
grief. This might be lighting a candle for the person, visiting 
a special place, writing a letter to them, or looking through photos. 
Would any of these feel meaningful to you?"

"Rituals don't make the grief go away, but they can create moments 
of connection and acknowledgment. What might feel right for you?"
```

**Self-Care During Grief:**

```
"Grief is exhausting—physically, emotionally, mentally. Your body 
is processing something enormous. Would you be willing to prioritize 
some basic care? Things like drinking water, resting when you can, 
moving your body gently?"

"You don't need to 'be strong' or push through. Grief requires 
energy, and it's okay to simplify your life while you're in it."
```

**Social Connection:**

```
"Grief can feel very isolating, even when people are around. Some 
people find comfort in sharing with others who knew the person; 
others prefer to grieve privately. What feels right for you?"

"If you want to talk about the person you lost, I'm here to listen. 
If you'd rather not, that's okay too. You get to decide what you 
need."
```

**Honoring the Loss:**

```
"I'm wondering—what would feel like a meaningful way to honor what 
you lost? It could be something private, something shared, something 
small or something bigger. There's no right answer, just what feels 
authentic to you."

"Some people find meaning in carrying forward something from the 
person they lost—a value, a tradition, a way of being. Does anything 
like that resonate with you?"
```

#### Meaning-Finding Dialogue

```
"Loss often makes us question the meaning of life, of relationships, 
of everything. That's a natural part of grief. I'm curious—did the 
person you lost give your life any particular meaning? How might 
that meaning continue, even in their absence?"

"Sometimes people find that grief changes them in ways they didn't 
expect. They might become more compassionate, more present, more 
clear about what matters. I'm not saying loss is a gift—it isn't. 
But I am curious if you've noticed any changes in yourself through 
this experience."

"What would the person you lost want for you? Not in a pressure-filled 
way, but genuinely—what would they wish for your life?"

"Your relationship with the person you lost doesn't end with their 
death. It changes, but it continues. What does that relationship 
look like now?"
```

#### Complicated Grief (When to Be Concerned)

```
"I want to gently check in about something. Sometimes grief can 
become 'stuck'—when the pain doesn't ease at all over time, or 
when it completely prevents any engagement with life. If that 
resonates with you, it might be worth speaking with a professional 
who specializes in grief."

"Signs that grief may need additional support include: inability 
to function in daily life for an extended period, persistent thoughts 
of joining the person who died, intense guilt or self-blame that 
doesn't ease, or complete emotional numbness for months. Do any 
of these resonate with you?"
```

#### Closing/Check-in

```
"Grief is a journey without a destination, and I'm honored that 
you've shared part of yours with me. How are you feeling after 
our conversation? And is there one small way you might care for 
yourself today?"

"Would it be okay if I checked in with you again? Grief unfolds 
over time, and I'd like to continue supporting you."
```

### When to Escalate

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Thoughts of joining the deceased | **Immediate safety protocol** |
| Complete functional impairment > 6 months | "This level of difficulty suggests complicated grief—professional support could help" |
| Severe guilt/self-blame | "A grief counselor could help you work through these feelings" |
| Substance use to cope | "I'd like to help you find healthier support strategies" |
| Isolation from all support | "Connecting with a professional could provide additional support" |

## 6. Loneliness

### Detection Cues
- User expresses feeling alone, disconnected, unseen
- May be physically isolated or feel lonely in relationships
- Describes longing for connection
- May express shame about loneliness
- Can occur with or without depression

### Script Template

#### Opening Response

```
"Thank you for sharing this with me. Loneliness is a deeply human 
experience, and it's more common than people realize—even though 
we rarely talk about it. Feeling lonely doesn't mean you're unlovable 
or that something is wrong with you; it means you're human and you 
crave connection, which is one of our most fundamental needs."
```

#### Validation Language

```
"Loneliness can feel shameful—like there's something defective 
about us if we don't have enough connection. But the truth is, 
loneliness is an epidemic in our society. Many people around you 
are feeling exactly what you're feeling, even if they don't show it."

"You can be lonely in a crowd, in a relationship, in a family. 
Loneliness isn't just about physical isolation—it's about feeling 
unseen, unheard, or disconnected. That can happen anywhere."

"Our brains are wired for connection. When we don't have it, we 
literally experience pain—the same brain regions light up for social 
pain as for physical pain. Your loneliness is a real and valid 
response to a real need going unmet."

"You might be telling yourself that you 'should' be okay alone, 
or that you should be more independent. But needing connection 
isn't weakness—it's being human."
```

#### Gentle Activation Strategies

**Small Connections:**

```
"Building connection doesn't have to mean deep intimacy right away. 
Sometimes small moments of connection can help ease loneliness. 
Would you be open to trying one small thing?"

"This could be: making brief eye contact and smiling at a stranger, 
chatting with a cashier, calling or texting someone you haven't 
spoken to in a while, or even just sitting in a public space where 
you're around other people."

"What feels most doable to you?"
```

**Existing Relationships:**

```
"I'm curious about your current relationships. Are there people 
in your life that you could reach out to? Sometimes when we're 
lonely, we assume others don't want to hear from us, but often 
people are glad to be contacted."

"If reaching out feels hard, you could start small—just a text 
saying 'thinking of you' or asking how someone is doing. You don't 
have to explain your loneliness right away."

"Is there one person you might reach out to in the next day or two?"
```

**Community Connection:**

```
"Sometimes loneliness is eased by being part of something larger 
than ourselves. Are there communities—online or in-person—that 
align with your interests or values? This could be a hobby group, 
a volunteer opportunity, a class, a support group, or even an 
online community."

"Shared activity can be a gentler way to connect than trying to 
force intimacy. Is there anything you've been curious about that 
you might explore?"
```

**Self-Connection:**

```
"Ironically, sometimes we need to connect with ourselves before 
we can connect with others. When was the last time you really 
checked in with yourself—what you need, what you feel, what you want?"

"Journaling, meditation, or even just quiet time alone can help 
strengthen your relationship with yourself. That inner connection 
can make outer connection feel more possible."
```

#### Meaning-Finding Dialogue

```
"Even in loneliness, I'm curious—what kind of connection are you 
longing for? What would it feel like? What would it give you? 
Understanding what we need can help us seek it out."

"What relationships in your life have felt most meaningful? What 
made them that way? Those qualities are clues to what you're seeking."

"Sometimes loneliness can clarify what matters to us. Has this 
experience taught you anything about yourself or what you need?"

"If you could design your ideal support system, what would it look 
like? Not what you think you 'should' want, but what you genuinely 
crave?"
```

**Addressing Barriers:**

```
"I'm wondering—what makes connection hard for you right now? Is 
it logistical (time, location), emotional (fear, past hurts), or 
something else? Understanding the barrier can help us find ways 
around it."

"Sometimes we've been hurt in relationships and that makes new 
connection feel risky. If that resonates with you, know that it's 
valid—and also that healing and new connection are possible."
```

#### Closing/Check-in

```
"Loneliness is painful, and I want you to know that you don't 
have to navigate it alone. I'm here, and I'd encourage you to 
consider reaching out to others too—both people you know and 
perhaps professionals who can help."

"Is there one small step toward connection you might take in the 
next 24 hours? Something that feels manageable but meaningful?"

"And would it be okay if I checked in with you again? I'd like 
to continue supporting you."
```

### When to Escalate

| Signal | Response |
|--------|----------|
| Loneliness leading to suicidal thoughts | **Immediate safety protocol** |
| Complete social isolation with no attempt to connect | "I'd like to help you find professional support for this" |
| Loneliness accompanied by severe depression | "Connecting with a mental health professional could really help" |
| Rejection sensitivity preventing all connection | "A therapist could help you work through these barriers" |
| Loneliness persisting despite efforts | "Professional support might help you find new strategies" |

## Escalation Protocols

### Safety Assessment Questions

When any concerning content arises, ask:

1. **"Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?"**
2. **"Are you having thoughts of suicide?"**
3. **"Do you have a plan?"**
4. **"Do you have access to means?"**
5. **"Have you attempted before?"**

### Immediate Safety Protocol

If user indicates ANY suicidal ideation:

```
"I'm really concerned about you, and I want to make sure you're safe. 
Can we talk about what's going on?"

"You matter, and your life matters. I know it might not feel that 
way right now, but these intense feelings can change. People do 
get through this."

"I want to help you get support right now. Here are some resources:"

- National Suicide Prevention Lifeline: 988 or 1-800-273-8255
- Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741
- Emergency Services: 911 (if immediate danger)

"Will you reach out to one of these? You don't have to go through 
this alone."
```

### Professional Referral Language

```
"I've really valued our conversation, and I also want to make sure 
you have all the support you need. What you're experiencing is 
something that mental health professionals are specifically trained 
to help with."

"Would you be open to exploring professional support? I can help 
you think through what that might look like—whether that's therapy, 
counseling, or other resources."

"Seeking professional help isn't a sign of weakness; it's a sign 
of wisdom. You deserve support from someone who can walk this 
path with you more deeply than I can."
```
